BibAV: A Replicable Linked-Data Infrastructure for Participatory Public-Domain Digital Libraries

Digitization has not eliminated infrastructural asymmetries in access to print heritage. Public-domain works frequently remain mediated by proprietary interfaces that constrain downloadability, obscure metadata inconsistencies, and centralize preservation within corporate systems. These conditions disproportionately affect Global South corpora, whose visibility and reuse depend on infrastructures beyond their governance. This paper introduces BibAV (Biblioteca Abierta Venezolana) as a methodological framework for constructing open, participatory, and preservation-oriented digital libraries capable of operating across such constraints.

Initially instantiated through a corpus of Venezuelan public-domain printed works, the project is designed as a transferable model applicable to any authority-controlled bibliographic domain. It integrates scripted extraction of author data from Wikidata, reconciliation of identifiers through VIAF, and automated interrogation of the Google Books API to identify and verify genuinely downloadable materials. These processes operate within a linked open data ecosystem that enables authority normalization and metadata interoperability across platforms. Records are curated in a collaborative Zotero group library functioning as a shared backend, and published via MOREL, an open-source interface developed for dynamic filtering, structured exploration, and reuse. The entire workflow is version-controlled, publicly documented, and supported by open repositories to ensure auditability and long-term traceability.

Audience involvement is implemented through editable metadata structures and distributed governance of bibliographic records. Contributors participate in authority correction, corpus expansion, and documentation practices. Curatorial decisions also address structural underrepresentation by prioritizing the inclusion and visibility of historically marginalized authors within public-domain collections. The infrastructure is designed for pedagogical replication: classrooms and research groups may fork, adapt, and extend the collection within their own collaborative environments.

Currently comprising over 322 publicly accessible titles and undergoing continuous expansion through daily curatorial workflows, BibAV operates as a live research environment. Its contribution to humanistic inquiry lies in enabling reusable corpus construction, supporting bibliographic and intellectual-historical analysis, and establishing a distributed preservation strategy grounded in open standards, linked data integration, and collectively maintainable infrastructures. The model offers a sustainable pathway for securing and activating public-domain cultural heritage under conditions of infrastructural dependency.
